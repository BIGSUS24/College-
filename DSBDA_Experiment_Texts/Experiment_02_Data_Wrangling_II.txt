Experiment: Data Wrangling II

Theory

Data wrangling II extends the basic cleaning workflow by focusing on missing data, outliers, and
transformation of the academic performance dataset. Student datasets are especially useful because
they often contain a mix of numeric and categorical features such as marks, dates, attendance, and
placement counts. They also tend to contain realistic imperfections, which makes them ideal for
demonstrating why preprocessing matters before any statistical conclusion is drawn.

The first issue is missing and inconsistent values. A missing value may not always mean a completely
blank cell. Sometimes it is hidden in the form of an invalid string, a zero used as a placeholder,
or a value outside the logical range of the column. For instance, a date written in the wrong year
or a score greater than the maximum possible score is inconsistent even if it is not technically
null. The analyst must scan the dataset carefully and decide whether the issue should be corrected,
replaced, or removed.

After the missing values are addressed, the next task is detecting outliers. Outliers are
observations that sit far away from the main cluster of data. In an academic dataset, they may
appear as unusually high marks, extremely low attendance, or strange placement counts. Some outliers
are genuine and represent exceptional students, while others are errors introduced during data
entry. Because outliers can distort mean values and statistical measures, they must be handled with
care.

There are several standard ways to detect outliers. Box plots provide a visual summary of quartiles
and make extreme values easy to notice. Scatter plots can reveal points that break away from a
pattern. Z-scores measure how far each point is from the mean in standard deviation units, and the
interquartile range method identifies values outside a robust lower and upper bound. Among these,
the IQR method is especially popular because it works well even when the data is not perfectly
normal.

Once outliers are identified, the analyst must choose a treatment. Removing them completely is
simple but can reduce the amount of useful information in the dataset. Capping or flooring values
keeps the record but limits the influence of extremes. Median replacement is another common approach
because the median is less sensitive to unusual values. The correct method depends on whether the
outlier is a data error or a true but rare observation.

Data transformation is used when the shape or scale of a variable makes analysis difficult. For
example, a column that is heavily right-skewed can be transformed using a log or square-root
function to make its distribution more balanced. Transformation can also bring variables to a
similar scale so that comparisons become easier. This is useful when one variable has very small
values and another has very large values. The main goal is to make the data easier to interpret and
more suitable for statistical analysis.

The academic performance dataset is a good example of how preprocessing affects later results. If
missing values remain, the analysis may become incomplete. If outliers remain untreated, averages
and models may become misleading. If transformation is not applied where needed, the variable may
remain too skewed for good interpretation. Therefore, the experiment demonstrates how cleaning,
outlier handling, and transformation work together as a single preparation pipeline.

This assignment also helps students understand that preprocessing decisions are not random. Each
action must have a reason. Dropping, replacing, or transforming data should always be chosen based
on the structure of the dataset and the objective of the analysis. This careful reasoning is what
separates proper data work from casual editing.

Conclusion

The experiment shows that academic data must be cleaned systematically before it can be trusted. By
handling missing values, detecting outliers, and applying transformations, the dataset becomes more
stable and meaningful. This prepares it for statistical analysis and improves the quality of every
later result.

Additional Discussion

A careful analyst also checks whether the outliers are truly wrong or simply uncommon. For example,
a very high placement score may be unusual but still valid if the student performed exceptionally
well. Removing such a point blindly could damage the analysis. That is why visual inspection and
domain knowledge matter. The data cleaning process should support the story told by the data rather
than destroy meaningful exceptions.

Transformation is often underestimated, but it can change the usability of a variable completely. A
heavily skewed column may produce unstable averages and make comparisons difficult. After a log
transformation, the values may become easier to compare and the distribution may become more
symmetric. This helps many statistical methods work better because they assume roughly balanced or
normal-looking input. Transformation is therefore not only cosmetic; it often improves the
mathematical behavior of the data.

In educational datasets, the cleaned values can later be used to study performance trends, predict
placement outcomes, or compare different batches of students. That makes the dataset more than a
classroom example. It becomes a model for real analysis where decisions may depend on accurate and
fair interpretation of student records. Good preprocessing ensures that those decisions are based on
solid data rather than accidental noise.

Final Notes

In practice, outlier handling is often the point where judgment matters most. A value that looks
strange may still be correct, and removing it without checking can weaken the analysis. This is why
the analyst should always compare visual evidence with the meaning of the column. The goal is not to
force all values into a narrow pattern, but to keep the data honest and usable.

Transformation is useful because many methods behave better when variables are closer to a normal
shape or a comparable scale. A log transformation can compress very large values and reveal
structure that was hidden before. A standardized or normalized variable can also improve comparison
across features. These adjustments are simple, but they often improve the quality of the final
interpretation.

This experiment is therefore a complete example of preprocessing. It shows how cleaning, detection,
and transformation are linked. Each step supports the next one, and together they make the dataset
more trustworthy for later analysis.
